A Living Book on Attention, Regulation, and Civil Exchange

Thomas J. Hafner · February 2026

Meet Tom Read the First Essay

This project is born from my desire to better understand my own mind.

I believe the foundation of all systems is communication.
How we communicate internally shapes our personal system.
How we communicate externally shapes our society.

How are our systems doing?

Let’s explore this together — calmly, intentionally, and without judgment.

This project will move slowly, but it will remain free. Support is optional.

What This Is

Flip the Discourse is a structured, evolving book built in public.
It is not a reaction feed.
It is not a political platform.
It is a long-form inquiry into how human systems regulate — and how they fail.
Chapters will be released sequentially and revised over time as understanding deepens.
Substack will function as the working kitchen — where ideas are drafted, tested, and refined before entering the book.
Audio narration will accompany each chapter and will be available via Apple Podcasts and Spotify, with links archived here.
Social channels (YouTube, Instagram, Facebook, X, TikTok) will serve distribution — not argument.

Why This Project Exists

Modern discourse often feels compressed, reactive, and adversarial. When information channels narrow, perception narrows. When perception narrows, tension hardens.

This project explores whether disciplined attention — both individual and collective — can widen those channels again. The aim is not victory. The aim is clarity.

Compassion for the Human Condition

Very few of us wake up intending to cause harm. Yet we see increasing tension within individuals and across institutions.

Flip the Discourse explores how patterns of attention, regulation, and communication shape perception — and how narrowing perception can destabilize both personal agency and public systems.

This project develops a simple working thesis:

Regulation → Metacognition → Agency → Civil Discourse → System Stability

The Structure of the Book

  • Tension — Recognition of internal and social strain.
  • Attention — The gateway to agency.
  • Metacognition — Thinking about thinking — and regaining choice.
  • Dysregulation — How stress narrows systems.
  • Polemic Discourse — How adversarial exchange shuts down information flow.
  • Social Learning — How beliefs propagate and harden.
  • Civil Exchange — Disciplined dialogue as regulatory practice.
  • Meaning & Agency — Responsibility without self-righteousness.

This architecture is intentional. Each chapter builds upon the last.
